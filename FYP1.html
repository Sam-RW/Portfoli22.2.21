Analysis of the Concrete Industry with regards to End of Life Concrete Carbonation

Sam Riddell-Webster Word Count: 2743


Abstract

This paper outlines the material flows and associated carbon emissions within the concrete industry, followed by a brief assessment of the life cycle stages. This was completed in order to asses the viability of the industry, for a large scale implementation of end of life concrete carbonation.


This novel approach to carbon capture was assessed and the extent of its carbon sequestering potential determined. Its was concluded that an end of life carbonation system could provide a efficient alternative to current carbon capture and storage methods while closing the life cycle loop in the industry.

Concrete is the second most commonly utilised substance in the world by mass, second only to water1. Though when compared to other building materials, concrete has a low embodied carbon cost, around of 0.15kg C02/kg. Despite this, the vast quantities used make it responsible for around 8% of global anthropogenic Carbon emissions.


Initial Project brief:

3

Optimising the carbonation of recycled concrete - concrete absorbs C02 during it’s life span but when recycled it is only partially re-carbonated. How can the recycling process be developed to maximise carbonation of concrete?


Despite being a major contributor to carbon emissions, concrete could be used as significant carbon sink. This is due to a reaction that occurs under environmental conditions, whereby concrete absorbs carbon dioxide and stores it in a stable, physical form. This phenomenon has been well documented and been common knowledge in the industry for over a decade. Despite this, there are no examples of recycled concrete carbonation (RCC) at a scale proportional to the size of the industry.


In this paper, the scale of the product and emission production of the cement industry is explored. Background research was also conducted to provide a firm understanding of the industry. Carbonation was then assessed for its feasibility at scale and compared to some of the industry standards of today.

Problem Identification


Problem identification

Scope


Global approaches to cement and concrete manufacture vary significantly in a range of factors including material selection, approaches to recycling and scale of operations. As a result, the research and data in this paper focuses on operations within the United Kingdom. Despite the focus of the brief on the end of life, it was deemed necessary to consider the entire life cycle. As concrete both produces and absorbs C02, the full product life must be understood in order to determine whether a life-cycle based approach could be the answer to optimising concrete carbonation. There are a range of cement substitutes available than could offer lower carbon alternatives to traditional Portland cement (PC), however they are not commonly used and so hold little relevance to the built environment of today. As a result, they were considered outside the scope of this paper. There are a multitude of techniques for reducing emissions in the industry. In order to remain focused on the brief, only those directly related to carbonation were considered.


Goal

The aim of this study was to explore the problem space: The carbon emissions in the cement industry. Current carbon reduction methods and the scale of production must be understood in order to develop a feasible, scalable solution to the carbonation of end of life concrete. I was also important to determine whether there was sufficient reasoning to back a carbon capture and usage (CCU) approach, over a carbon capture and storage (CCS) approach. The former involves using the RCC to produce new concrete while the latter would follow current down-cycle routes for end of life concrete.

Feasibility at Industrial Scale

Annually, it is estimated around 40 million tons of concrete are processed through demolition 9 6.

For implementation of end of life carbonation to be feasible on a national scale, the rate of carbonation must be drastically increased. Storage for this quantity of material for a months or even years, would be impractical and expensive.

Optimum Conditions


Resent research 1 10 11 has shown that the complete carbonation of recycled concrete is not only feasible, but achievable at relatively low temperatures and pressures, unlike other CCS methods. The results of this research can be seen in figure 12, which shows the carbonation of cement paste over a 15-hour period.


The most critical requirement for the carbonation process, perhaps unsurprisingly, is an abundance of C02. Given that the vast majority of carbon emissions from the concrete industry are generated from a single process, it seems highly rational to implement a carbonation system at this source. There are draw backs to this such as the transportation of end of life concrete, back to the manufacturing plant, but the transport emissions are outweighed by the savings form clinker substitution, see below.


It is clear from figure 12 that the most significant variable to achieve complete carbonation was the particle size. Complete carbonation was achieved for the fine sample with particles < 90 μm, but not at

<2mm. Current recycling methods, as highlighted previously, do not grind concrete to anywhere near this level of coarseness. This would require extra energy input, though the emissions resulting from the crushing process are 80 times smaller than the C02 absorbed through the carbonation process 3.

It should also be noted that these experiments were carried out using a hydrated cement paste. In order to recreate these conditions at scale, all cement would need to be separated from the aggregates that make up the majority of the concrete. This would require the development of recycling technologies well beyond those used today.


The other significant variables were temperature and pressure. As shown in figure 12, the variation of pressure had little to no correlation to the results. Therefore, a scaled operation could perform effectively at atmospheric conditions without the need for applied pressure.


Complete carbonation can also be achieved at relatively low temperatures, around 80 °C for the fine particle case. This is well below the exit temperatures of flu gases from a rotary kiln after the waste heat recovery system 12, and so should not pose a problem to full scale implementation. This use of currently wasted energy and the proximity to the emissions are both strong arguments for why the carbonation process should be carried out at the manufacturing plant rather than at a demolition site or waste processing plant.

As the primary source of emissions are produced through clinker manufacturing, a well-practiced method for reducing the carbon emissions of cement is to reduce the clinker to cement ratio. This is generally achieved through the use of clinker substitutes; some common examples include:


  • Fly ash, coal power plants (WP)


  • Ground Granulated Blast furnace slag (GGBS), steel industry (WP)


  • Limestone raw material (RM)


  • Gypsum (RM) 17


(RM) = Raw material, (WP) = Waste Product


The waste products are favourable as they are by products of other processes, therefore are considered to have no carbon emissions. Any associated emissions have already been considered in the manufacturing process that produces them.

The use of fillers affects the physical properties of the concrete, in most cases reducing compressive strength. However, research shows that the limited use of fillers is more closely linked to supply than physical properties 14.


Fly ash is sourced from the exhaust gases of coal power plants, which have historically made up a large proportion of the UKs power supply. As a result, the UK have had surplus of Fly ash over the past 30 years 19. However, our reduced reliance on coal power generation is predicted to lead to a depletion of fly ashes reserves by as early as 2023 19.

Similarly, a shortage of UK GGBS is predicted due to the reduction of steel manufacturing. Both, these materials are still available on international markets, but this would lead to an increase in price which could cause cement manufacturers to look for other alternatives. Fly ash is not well suited for long distance freight as it has a very low density, resulting in higher shipping per ton. This predicted shortages of current fillers suggest the market is well placed to accept alternative solutions.

Effect of Carbonation on Clinker substitution

Increasing the percentage of substitution materials in cement reduces the net carbon emissions as less clinker needs to be produced. However, the same process reduces the strength class of the cement which increases the amount of cement needed in the concrete mix to achieve the same strength, thereby negating the savings of using more filler

14. To avoid this problem, cement with high filler content is only used for concrete with limited structural importance.


Experimental data has shown that the use of RCC as a substitute“has a positive effect on the compressive strength of concrete” 11. It also reduced the time taken for the cement to develop structural integrity which is an important consideration, as it defines how quickly structural supports can be removed during construction 18.


Given the improvements shown by the addition of RCC, it is reasonable to assume that RCC could be used in a range of cement types including CEM I, (>95% clinker). Therefore, the use of every ton of RCC could directly correspond to a reduction in the need for clinker of the same weight. The average emissions of clinker are 86.5g of C02/ 100g 1. When this is added to the emissions sequestered by RCC, the carbon emissions could be reduced by 114.5g per kg of concrete processed, carbonated and used as clinker substitute. It should be noted that 1kg of recycled concrete would lead to 100g approx. of filler as aggregate material would have to be removed.


Assuming all waste concrete is processed to produce RCC, the annual supply could equate to 4 times the annual availability of fly ash 20.


Conclusion


This research has shown that the implementation of carbonation of end of life concrete is feasible at production scale. It has been demonstrated that this process could be completed under conditions that would require minimal energy input. Atmospheric pressure and a temperature of 80°C could be achieve with no energy input if the carbonation was completed at the cement manufacturing plant. This is due to an exhaust gas temperature of 480°C.


Advancement is needed in the processing of demolition waste in order to at least partially isolate the cement past from the existing concrete.


Once carbonated, the RCC can be used as a clinker substitute. This integration increases the strength of the concrete and so represents a value addition. Expenses for the processing of this material can also be offset against the cost of purchasing alternative clinker substitute material. Use of RCC as a clinker substitute could also lead to significant savings due to reduced reliance of fly ash and GGBS from overseas.


The true benefit of carbonated cement will only be realised if it is used as a method of carbon capture and use. To utilize the full carbon reducing potential of this material, it must be used to offset the clinker production as well as sequester C02 from the atmosphere.
